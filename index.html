Key Insight: Profound disconnect between the intended use of the CareAI system and how it was actually implemented in employment decisions.

The case study illustrates how healthcare organizations initially deployed an AI system designed to promote wellness, but then inappropriately used this system to make consequential employment decisions without proper safeguards. The blueprint clearly shows how the hospital made Dr. Johnson's "scheduling preferences and leadership eligibility contingent on compliance with AI recommendations" without adequate human oversight or accommodation considerations.

This misalignment is at the core of the compliance failure, with the lowest compliance scores across all stakeholders occurring during the "Adverse Employment Decision" phase. This represents a critical blind spot: organizations failing to recognize when an AI system transitions from an assistive tool to a system making "consequential decisions" as defined by Colorado's law.

The case demonstrates how AI systems developed for one purpose (wellness monitoring) can evolve into high-risk systems under regulatory definitions when their outputs are used to make material decisions about people's careers and opportunities - triggering legal requirements for impact assessments, human review processes, and notification protocols that the organization failed to implement.

Key Observations:

  • Initial compliance levels were moderate to poor across most responsible entities, showing limited preparation for AI regulation.
  • The adverse employment decision phase shows the lowest compliance scores, representing the critical incident that triggered awareness.
  • All compliance-obligated stakeholders show improvement in the resolution phase, indicating effective remediation efforts.
  • Legal Team shows the most dramatic improvement from initial deployment to resolution (4 → 9).
  • Technical teams started with higher baseline compliance but still had significant room for improvement.
  • Compliance ratings improve systematically after regulatory intervention, suggesting the effectiveness of enforcement actions.
  • The case illustrates how SB24-205 places obligations on organizations deploying and developing AI, not on individuals affected by AI systems.

SB24-205 Compliance Improvement Opportunities:

  1. Proactive risk assessment protocols before initial deployment
  2. Enhanced training on algorithmic bias for all stakeholders
  3. Implementation of human review processes for all consequential decisions
  4. Clearer documentation of how AI recommendations should be used in employment contexts
  5. Formal appeals processes for individuals affected by AI-influenced decisions
