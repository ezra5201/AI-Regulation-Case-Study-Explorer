Key Insight: Multiple critical disconnects between known AI limitations and continued use in high-stakes employment decisions

The TalentView-FinSoft case demonstrates a particularly troubling pattern: continued use of AI systems for high-stakes employment decisions despite documented knowledge of their limitations with specific protected groups. This creates multiple points of potential liability under Colorado SB24-205.

Three critical disconnects stand out in this case study:

1. Knowledge vs. Action Gap: FinSoft had prior, direct knowledge that automated speech recognition (ASR) systems couldn't accurately evaluate J.M.'s speech due to her deaf accent. Rather than address this technological limitation, they simply modified her job duties. Yet they later required her to use TalentView - a system using similar ASR technology - for a promotion assessment, despite having been explicitly warned about its accessibility issues.

2. Promotion Criteria vs. Assessment Method Misalignment: The case shows a stark disconnect between J.M.'s demonstrated qualifications (5 years of successful employment with positive feedback and bonuses) and the method chosen to evaluate her promotion readiness (an automated video interview known to perform poorly with deaf speakers). This raises questions about whether the assessment method could "accurately reflect the knowledge, skills, and abilities" of the applicant as required by anti-discrimination laws.

3. Accommodation Request vs. Implementation Failure: When J.M. requested CART captioning as an accommodation, FinSoft incorrectly claimed TalentView had built-in subtitling. This demonstrates a failure to verify accommodation effectiveness before denying the requested accommodation - a critical compliance issue that affects both companies.

Under Colorado's SB24-205, these disconnects suggest potential violations of both developer and deployer obligations, particularly the requirement to use "reasonable care to protect consumers from any known or reasonably foreseeable risks of algorithmic discrimination." The feedback J.M. received after rejection - focused on communication style and suggesting she "practice active listening" - provides compelling evidence that the algorithmic assessment evaluated factors directly related to her disability.

SB24-205 Compliance Improvement Opportunities:

  1. Comprehensive Documentation: Both developer and deployer should document known limitations of AI systems with specific demographic groups, including deaf users and non-white users.
  2. Effective Accommodations Process: Establish clear procedures for requesting, evaluating, and implementing accommodations for AI systems, with verification of effectiveness.
  3. Alternative Assessment Methods: Develop alternative evaluation protocols when AI systems have known limitations with specific protected groups.
  4. Human Oversight Requirements: Implement mandatory human review of all AI-influenced employment decisions, especially for candidates from protected groups.
  5. Inclusive Testing Protocols: Test AI systems with diverse users including those with disabilities and from various racial backgrounds before deployment.
  6. Cross-Functional Compliance Teams: Ensure accessibility experts are involved in AI procurement and deployment decisions.
  7. Disparate Impact Analysis: Regularly analyze outcomes of AI-based assessments to identify potential discriminatory patterns.
