Key Insight: Multiple critical disconnects between known AI limitations and continued use in high-stakes employment decisions

The TalentVue-AcmeSoft case demonstrates a particularly troubling pattern: continued use of AI systems for high-stakes employment decisions despite documented knowledge of their limitations with specific protected groups. This creates multiple points of potential liability under Colorado SB24-205.

Three critical disconnects stand out in this case study:

1. Knowledge vs. Action Gap: AcmeSoft had prior, direct knowledge that automated speech recognition (ASR) systems couldn't accurately evaluate J.D.'s speech due to her deaf accent. Rather than address this technological limitation, they simply modified her job duties. Yet they later required her to use TalentVue - a system using similar ASR technology - for a promotion assessment, despite having been explicitly warned about its accessibility issues.

2. Promotion Criteria vs. Assessment Method Misalignment: The case shows a stark disconnect between J.D.'s demonstrated qualifications (5 years of successful employment with positive feedback and bonuses) and the method chosen to evaluate her promotion readiness (an automated video interview known to perform poorly with deaf speakers). This raises questions about whether the assessment method could "accurately reflect the knowledge, skills, and abilities" of the applicant as required by anti-discrimination laws.

3. Accommodation Request vs. Implementation Failure: When J.D. requested CART captioning as an accommodation, AcmeSoft incorrectly claimed TalentVue had built-in subtitling. This demonstrates a failure to verify accommodation effectiveness before denying the requested accommodation - a critical compliance issue that affects both companies.

Under Colorado's SB24-205, these disconnects suggest potential violations of both developer and deployer obligations, particularly the requirement to use "reasonable care to protect consumers from any known or reasonably foreseeable risks of algorithmic discrimination." The feedback J.D. received after rejection - focused on communication style and suggesting she "practice active listening" - provides compelling evidence that the algorithmic assessment evaluated factors directly related to her disability.

Key Observations:

  • The lowest compliance scores (1-2) occur during the automated interview experience phase, indicating serious failures by both AcmeSoft and TalentVue.
  • AcmeSoft shows particularly poor compliance during the interview phase (score: 1), having incorrectly claimed capabilities that didn't exist and failed to verify accommodation effectiveness.
  • AcmeSoft had experienced firsthand issues with automated speech recognition systems' limitations with J.D.'s deaf accent yet continued to use similar technology for promotion decisions.
  • TalentVue shows equally poor compliance in the automated interview phase (score: 1), particularly in failing to provide accessibility features that were claimed to exist.
  • The AcmeSoft Accessibility Team shows the most troubling compliance failure, with a score of 1 during promotion application, as they had explicit knowledge of potential issues yet took no apparent action.
  • Compliance scores generally decline from initial employment through the automated interview process, indicating a deterioration in responsibility as the process progressed.
  • The intersection of disability and racial factors creates a particularly complex compliance scenario that neither company adequately addressed.
  • Management showed slightly better compliance in early phases (scores 5 and 4) but failed to maintain this standard when the promotion process began.
  • Colorado SB24-205's specific requirements for high-risk AI systems in employment decisions were consistently violated throughout the promotion process.

SB24-205 Compliance Improvement Opportunities:

  1. Comprehensive Documentation: Both developer and deployer should document known limitations of AI systems with specific demographic groups, including deaf users and non-white users.
  2. Effective Accommodations Process: Establish clear procedures for requesting, evaluating, and implementing accommodations for AI systems, with verification of effectiveness.
  3. Alternative Assessment Methods: Develop alternative evaluation protocols when AI systems have known limitations with specific protected groups.
  4. Human Oversight Requirements: Implement mandatory human review of all AI-influenced employment decisions, especially for candidates from protected groups.
  5. Inclusive Testing Protocols: Test AI systems with diverse users including those with disabilities and from various racial backgrounds before deployment.
  6. Cross-Functional Compliance Teams: Ensure accessibility experts are involved in AI procurement and deployment decisions.
  7. Disparate Impact Analysis: Regularly analyze outcomes of AI-based assessments to identify potential discriminatory patterns.
       
           

Step-by-Step Roadmap to Establish Compliance with the Colorado AI Act

           
           

Over the next year, TalentVue's stakeholders will work to establish compliance with the Colorado AI Act amidst ongoing demands. The project will kick off with the CEO initiating the compliance effort and quickly assigning a project lead, aiming for a focused approach given limited bandwidth, ensuring resources are allocated pragmatically and accountability is clear from the start.

The CTO will rapidly assess current AI systems, pinpoint key gaps and risks, and develop a workable technical compliance roadmap. This roadmap will guide the necessary technical adjustments, leading to a final compliance review to document findings and essential improvements, all while balancing other critical technical priorities.

The CMO will swiftly align the marketing strategy with compliance needs, develop a concise communication strategy, and get it implemented. Ongoing monitoring will aim to keep the strategy on track, with adjustments made as urgently needed amidst other marketing imperatives.

The Legal team will review the Colorado AI Act requirements, draft essential compliance policies, conduct focused training sessions, and monitor adherence as time allows. They'll also update policies pragmatically to address any legal issues that surface, while managing their broader caseload.

The Executive Team will define core roles and responsibilities, coordinate cross-functional efforts efficiently, and track progress during implementation, addressing critical roadblocks and reporting on compliance status as part of their regular oversight, adjusting the strategy when absolutely necessary.

The Governance Committee will be formed to oversee the compliance implementation, developing key governance policies, monitoring adherence where feasible, and evaluating the effectiveness of these policies, reporting on outcomes and essential improvements within their scheduled activities.

Investors and Board Relations will be kept informed about the compliance project from the start through necessary updates and inclusion in relevant discussions, ensuring they are aware of progress and addressing urgent concerns. The final compliance outcomes will be reported, highlighting key successes and lessons learned under the given constraints.

Key milestones will include the initial project setup, the development of a practical technical compliance roadmap, the implementation of essential communication strategies, and the establishment of core governance policies. These milestones will demonstrate TalentVue's focused effort and intent to achieve compliance with the Colorado AI Act, ensuring all stakeholders are informed and engaged as efficiently as possible throughout the process.
